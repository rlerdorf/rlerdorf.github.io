Complexity is easy. Simplicity is hard. This is true in code, in prose, and in most things worth doing well.

Every abstraction you add is a bet that the problem it solves is more common than the confusion it introduces. Sometimes that bet pays off. Often it doesn't.

The cost of abstraction

When I look at software systems that have aged well, they almost always share a trait: the abstractions are thin and the primitives are exposed. The layers exist to organize, not to hide.

When I look at systems that became unmaintainable, the opposite is usually true. Someone built a framework on top of a framework on top of a framework, and now nobody can trace a request from the outside world to the line of code that handles it.

A useful heuristic
